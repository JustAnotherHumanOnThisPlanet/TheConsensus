A New Lens for the World
We all see the world through different perspectives. Our eyes act like cameras, capturing snapshots of experiences and the laws that govern the universe. Each person carries a lens and an individual way of viewing what exists. Throughout humanity’s history, we’ve often examined problems through lenses shaped by religion, race, skin color, or nationality. But now is the time to shift our focus and look at the world through a new lens,one that transcends these divisions.
The Keyword: Lens
Now this is something important that you’ll find across the entire book the keyword lens.
Everyone has a view. A person living remotely as part of a prehistoric tribe might see the world differently than someone living in a developed country. The most important thing is that both have a lens of their own, and both matter greatly. Each perspective allows us to look at a problem and arrive at consensus based on their judgment.I request you to provide your lens to humanity to help us look at problems and reach a general consensus. Every lens matters. Let’s explore this through an example.
The Human Lens and Intuition
Many religious communities view mental health struggles like depression, anxiety, or addiction strictly through a spiritual lens. They see these conditions as a lack of faith, a spiritual weakness, a punishment, or demonic influence. The standard advice is simply to "pray harder" or fast more.
Human Lens - This lens looks at the profound, silent suffering of the individual. It recognizes that the human brain is a biological organ prone to illness, just like the heart or lungs. It understands that telling a suffering person they simply lack faith breeds intense guilt, isolation, and often leads to preventable self-harm.
This proves that we are focusing on the wrong things as humans. Human intuition should always be placed above everything else when making judgments and decisions. My human intuition clearly tells me that there are Christians, Muslims, Chinese, Americans, Mexicans in our world but in truth, that’s an illusion. We are humans first.
Many intelligent people on this planet who understand this message will contribute to this book. Some may contribute early, while others will join once they recognize the illusions and free themselves from these traps of life.
I ask for a promise from you. It’s a deep promise from one human to another: whenever you look at a problem from now on, look at it through the human lens first. Many of humanity’s problems will begin to fade as more and more people free themselves.
The Child’s Lens vs. The Adult’s Lens
If you ask a child how they would make the world a happier place, their innocence gives an answer very different from ours. A child might say, “I’d make chocolates free for everyone.” They wouldn’t limit it to just Americans,Australians or any one group.
Now compare that to a modern question: If I discover a new source of clean energy, should it be shared freely with all humanity, or restricted so only certain nations benefit? Why do we start thinking in terms of divisions as we grow older? What makes us lose that pure human lens?
Somewhere along the way, stereotypes, politics, and boundaries replace the simplicity of humanity. That’s the lens we need to reclaim if we want to build a better world.
We have endless stories from history and the modern era to look at from different lenses and build our consensus for an evolving world order.Now is the time to contribute to a better cause, to build a world that reflects humanity’s highest values.

The Concept of Neutral Lenses
The concept of neutral lenses is very important. When you put forward a neutral lens in reaching consensus, you begin to see a problem in a completely different way. You examine other lenses offered by others, but the solution to most problems often lies in these neutral lenses. By asking the right questions and applying these lenses, you start thinking beyond your current beliefs and discover new answers.
Sometimes consensus is difficult to achieve. If you remain adamant, you will never reach it. Be flexible and learn to accept different lenses like an adult. Right now, we waste so much energy and resources fighting over rights, rules, and laws. But what if we asked ourselves this crucial question: How would I design a world to tackle this problem?
In this imagined world, there are no barriers of religion, no discrimination based on skin color, and everyone is equal. All the factors that divide us in the real world are absent. We are one world, one family without borders. How would this change the picture of the problem?
Understanding Through Lenses - Example: Baptism vs Forced Conversion
Let’s understand this with an example. Baptism in Christianity is often described as a moment of profound beauty, a gentle act of faith where water symbolizes cleansing, renewal, and entry into a life with Christ. It is not something forced upon a person; rather, it is embraced freely, either by an individual who chooses to follow Jesus or by parents who lovingly dedicate their child within their tradition. The essence of baptism lies in voluntary faith, not compulsion.By contrast, forced conversions are a distortion of faith. History records times when political powers or colonial authorities imposed Christianity through coercion, stripping away the freedom that is central to belief. These acts are widely acknowledged as wrong and stand in opposition to the true spirit of the Gospel, which calls for love, respect, and free choice.
Now, you want to see what might fit into consensus. You just looked at this through a lens: Baptism versus Forced Conversion. Obviously, it is baptism. This is just an example to help your mind think in a neutral way. It is not about starting a cult, a religion, or discussing conversions. The focus is on the process of applying lenses to reach consensus.

It’s a beautiful idea, yet difficult to sustain in today’s capitalistic world, where hiring budgets are shaped by divisions.
